Determine if a language is regular from the first sight

Ask Question
up vote 3 down vote favorite
6

Is there a way to guess if a language is regular from the first sight? I.e. in order to choose proof methods, I have to have some hypothesis at first. Do you know any hints/patterns?

I need this to reduce time consumption: for instance, in order not to spend time on pumping lemma/Myhill–Nerode theorem, when in fact the language IS regular and I need to construct DFA/grammar.

The language is expressed as a set of all its words possible. For example:

L={(0)2n(10)3k+11m,n,k,m≥0},Σ={0,1}L={(0)2n(10)3k+11m,n,k,m≥0},Σ={0,1}

    Imagine that you’re reading a word one symbol at a time. How much do you have to remember as you go along in order to decide whether the word is in the language? Is that amount bounded, or can it be arbitrarily large (as with the language of words of the form 0n1n0n1n, for instance). If it’s bounded, you have a regular language. If it appears not to be bounded, you probably don’t have a regular language, though the impression that it’s unbounded may of course have been wrong. – Brian M. Scott Jan 19 '13 at 20:16
  • 2
    Unlike the pumping lemma, Myhill-Nerode works in both directions: A language LL is regular if and only if the number of Myhill-Nerode equivalence classes is finite (the equivalence relation being u∼vu∼v iff for every ww, the strings uwuw and vwvw are either both in LL or both not in LL.) – Ted Jan 19 '13 at 20:50

Easiest Round-off Trick!!!:

Step 1: Look at the exponents of the different alphabets.

Step 2: Is there any relation between the different exponents?

Step 3a: If there is NO relation between the exponents then the language is Regular and Context free. eg: L= anbmanbm , m,n >=0

Step 3b: If there are relations between the exponents, for instance, L=ambnL=ambn, and the relation can be m>n or m!=n or m< n. You will need one counter here to keep a count, inrementing it by 1 for n times for a and compare with b by decrementing by 1 for n-times and you get a 0 for a match(assuming, m=n). These languages are Not Regular but context free and accepted by a PDA.

Step 4: If you have more than one relation or need more than one counter, for instance, L=ambnckL=ambnck, m=n=k. Here, you need 2 such counters. First, count all a's and copy this count value to another counter. Then, compare with b by decrementing it n-times to get a 0. Again, decrementing the other counter n-times to compare it with c and accept the language if both the counters are 0. These languages are neither Regular nor Context Free but they are Context Sensitive and thus Recursive, although, all Recursive languages are not Context Sensitive

One simple criterion that works in many cases is that if you see only linear functions in the exponents (like 2n2n, 3k+13k+1, mm in your formula), and no variable appears more than once in the exponents, then the language is regular. This is because a language like {02n:n≥0}{02n:n≥0} corresponds to the regular expression (00)∗(00)∗. A rigorous proof of this rule (which also needs to be stated more precisely before it can be proved) uses closure properties of regular languages, which you should also know.
